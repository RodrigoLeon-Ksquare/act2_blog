A brief history about Ciudad del Carmen

Founded in pre-Hispanic times, Ciudad del Carmen was an important place that is due to connect the Aztec and Mayan civilizations. Between the 16th and 18th centuries, when the city of Campeche was a trade center between Spain and New Spain (Mexico), Ciudad del Carmen was habited by pirates and functioned as a port to repair ships and fly attacks against the Spanish people. A 1699 map of the Bay of Campeche indicates Isla del Carmen as two islands: Tieste I and Port Royal I. On the opposite side of the lagoon is a waterway marked Logwood Creek; this indicates that the residents were probably English and logging was another of their activities.


The city obtained its current name on July 16, 1717, in honor of the Virgen del Carmen, considered the protector of the island, when the Spanish forces, under the command of Alonso Felipe de Andrade, expelled the pirates from the island. Since then, every year at the end of July, Ciudad del Carmen becomes the very center of the regional social, cultural and religious festivals, in the fair that celebrates the protective virgin of the island.


The city is nicknamed "The Pearl of the Gulf". Ciudad del Carmen was a small city dedicated mainly to fishing until the 1970s when oil was discovered in the region; since then it has grown and developed substantially. To this day, Carmen is known as one of the best places to find seafood and "cochinita" in Mexico. In the early 1980s, the city could only be reached by ferries called "pangas" or small motor boats ("lanchas") that operated between Ciudad del Carmen and Zacatal; this changed with the construction of a causeway bridge to the mainland in the 1980s (to the east) and another in 1994 (to the west). The construction of the first bridge was motivated by the sinking of one of the island's pangas which resulted in the death of almost everyone on board. El Zacatal Bridge, built in 1994, is one of the longest in Latin America.
